5 SEO Signals to Watch in 2026

What's genuinely changing in Google's ranking criteria this year, and how to adjust your strategy without starting from scratch.

Every year brings a fresh batch of announcements that send marketing teams into a panic. Most have no real impact on a well-built site. But in 2026, five shifts genuinely change the game and deserve a strategy adjustment. Here they are, and more importantly, what to actually do about them.

1. AI-generated summaries are reshuffling the click

AI Overviews at the top of search results are absorbing more and more informational queries before the user ever clicks a link. For a chunk of traffic, ranking first isn't enough anymore — you also need to be the source cited inside the summary.

What to do: structure your content with clear, self-contained answers in the first few lines (definition, key figure, direct answer), use structured data (schema.org) to make automated reading easier, and focus your SEO effort on queries with transactional or local intent — far less absorbed by these summaries than purely informational ones.

2. E-E-A-T is now a survival criterion, not a bonus

Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust: these four letters aren't a box to tick in an SEO audit anymore, they directly condition a site's visibility, especially on high-stakes topics (health, finance, law, and increasingly marketing and business too).

What to do: feature real, identifiable authors with genuine expertise (not an anonymous "Admin" account), publish case studies and proprietary data rather than rephrasing what already exists elsewhere, and take care of "About" and legal pages, often neglected even though they're closely scrutinized.

3. Core Web Vitals are tightening, INP changes the game

Replacing First Input Delay with Interaction to Next Paint changed how Google measures a site's real responsiveness: it's no longer just the speed of the first click that counts, but the smoothness of every interaction throughout the visit.

What to do: audit third-party scripts (chat widgets, pop-ups, trackers) that weigh down interactivity, test your site on mobile under real conditions (not just on a recent desktop computer), and prioritize performance from the design stage rather than fixing it afterward.

4. Local search no longer happens only on Google

A growing share of local searches — especially among under-35s — happens directly on social media and maps apps. A Google Business Profile remains essential, but it's no longer the only entry point.

What to do: keep a Google Business Profile up to date with recent photos and review responses, maintain content on at least one social platform relevant to your audience, and keep information (address, hours, phone) consistent everywhere your business appears online.

5. Video and visual content are taking up more space in results

Video snippets and image search occupy a growing portion of results pages, especially for "how to" queries and searches for visual products or services.

What to do: don't neglect image naming and alt text, consider a short explainer video for your most strategic service pages, and make sure your existing video content is indexable (transcript, descriptive title).

None of these signals justify rebuilding everything. But taken together, they point to a clear trend: organic search increasingly rewards sites that prove real expertise and deliver a flawless technical experience, rather than those optimizing purely for the algorithm.
